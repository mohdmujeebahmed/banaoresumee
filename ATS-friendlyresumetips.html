A resume sent to fifty jobs unchanged loses to a resume rewritten for one. Recruiters and ATS software both reward specificity.

Stage 01 · The Resume

Write a resume that gets opened

Before a human ever reads it, your resume is usually scanned by software, then skimmed in seconds. These six habits fix the most common reasons strong candidates get filtered out.

Mirror the job posting

Pull 8–10 exact phrases — tools, titles, certifications — straight from the listing and weave them into your summary and bullets. Most resumes are matched on keyword overlap before a human opens the file.

Lead every bullet with a number

Not "managed social media" — "grew followers from 4K to 22K in 8 months, lifting lead volume 15%." Numbers are the fastest way to prove impact a stranger can't dismiss.

One page early, two pages later

Under 10 years of experience, fit it on one page. Past that, two pages is fine — but cut anything that doesn't support the specific role you want next.

Skip the objective, write a summary

Three lines, top of page: who you are, your strongest proof point, what you're aiming for. "Seeking a challenging opportunity" tells a recruiter nothing.

Know your region's conventions

In the US and UK, drop your photo, age, and marital status. In parts of Europe, the Middle East, and Asia, a photo and CV format are still standard. Match what's normal where you're applying.

Send the format they expect

PDF preserves formatting and reads cleanly in almost every modern ATS. If a posting specifically asks for Word, send a .docx — don't guess and hope.

Stage 02 · The Interview

Walk in ready to back it up

An interview is where your resume gets tested. These habits cover the preparation that consistently separates candidates with similar resumes.

Research three layers deep

Know the company's mission and recent news, the team you'd join, and — via LinkedIn — the person interviewing you. One specific reference shows you did the work.

Build five STAR stories

A conflict, a failure, a leadership moment, a tight deadline, a measurable win — each structured as Situation, Task, Action, Result. One good story can answer three different questions.

Script "tell me about yourself"

Use a simple bridge: where you've been, what you're doing now, why this role is the logical next step. Keep it under 90 seconds — it's a preview, not a memoir.

Treat video as a different medium

Test camera, mic, and lighting the day before. Set the camera at eye level, keep notes just below the lens — not on a script you're visibly reading — and close every other tab.

Ask questions that show intent

Skip salary in round one. Ask how success is measured in this role in the first six months, or what the biggest challenge facing the team is right now.

Follow up within 24 hours

A short thank-you note referencing one specific moment from the conversation keeps you visible — and gives you one more chance to add what you forgot to say.

Stage 03 · The Connection

Make the two documents agree

The candidates who get offers don't treat the resume and the interview as separate tasks — they treat the resume as the trailer for the interview.

Every bullet is an interview trailer

If you can't expand a line into a 90-second STAR story when asked, it doesn't belong on the page. Write bullets you can defend in detail.

Keep one master document

Maintain a single long file with every achievement and number you've produced. Pull 4–6 of the most relevant lines per application, keeping the underlying numbers identical everywhere.

Match your tone

If your resume reads data-driven and concise, your interview answers should too. Recruiters notice when the person in the room doesn't sound like the document they read.

Quick Reference

Two checklists, sixty seconds each

Run through these right before you hit submit, and again the night before you walk into the room.

Before you apply

  • Job description keywords are mirrored in your summary and top bullets
  • Every bullet leads with a number or a result
  • Contact info, LinkedIn, and portfolio link are current
  • File is named clearly: FirstName-LastName-Resume.pdf
  • Read it backwards, line by line, once — typos hide forwards
  • Saved in the file format the posting actually asks for

Before you interview

  • Five STAR stories ready, mapped to common questions
  • Three questions prepared to ask them
  • Company news, mission, and team researched
  • Outfit, route, or video setup confirmed the day before
  • Printed or digital copy of your resume on hand
  • Thank-you email drafted, ready to personalize after
FAQ

Common questions, answered straight

How long should a resume be in 2026?

One page if you have under 10 years of experience; two pages if you have more. Length matters less than relevance — every line should earn its place for the specific role you're applying to.

What's the difference between a resume and a CV?

In the US and Canada, "resume" means a 1–2 page summary tailored to a job. In the UK, Ireland, and much of Europe and Asia, "CV" is used for the same kind of document. The longer, multi-page academic CV used in research is a separate format.

What is an ATS, and how do I get past it?

An Applicant Tracking System (ATS) is software companies use to collect, sort, and search resumes before a recruiter sees them. Use standard section headings, avoid key info inside images or text boxes, and mirror the exact keywords from the posting.

How do I answer "tell me about yourself"?

Use a past-present-future bridge: a quick line on your background, what you're focused on now, and why this role is the next logical step. Aim for under 90 seconds.

Should I bring a printed resume to an interview?

Yes, even for a role you applied to online. Bring two or three clean copies — interviewers don't always have one printed, and it's a small signal of preparation.

How soon should I follow up after an interview?

Within 24 hours. A short, specific thank-you email referencing one real moment from the conversation keeps you visible and gives you one more chance to reinforce your fit.
