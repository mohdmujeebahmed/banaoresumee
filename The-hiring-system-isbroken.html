Why Companies Post Ghost Jobs

  • Market intelligence — gauging salary expectations and talent availability without committing to hire
  • Budget theater — role approved but budget not released yet; they "look" like they're hiring
  • Pipeline building — collecting CVs of strong candidates for future roles
  • Investor optics — posting jobs signals growth to investors and the market
  • Abandoned searches — the hiring manager left, the role was filled internally, or the team restructured

🕵️ How to Spot a Ghost Job

Posting is older than 30 days with no update. Company has recent layoff news. Vague job description. No specific team or manager mentioned. Suspiciously broad requirements. When in doubt: find an actual employee at the company on LinkedIn and ask directly. Real roles have real people behind them.

How to Access It

01

Alumni Network

Reach out to alumni from your college or previous companies. These are warm connections by default — most people are happy to help fellow graduates. Start with "I'd love 15 minutes to learn about your experience at [Company]."

02

Informational Interviews

Don't ask for a job. Ask for insight. "I admire the work your team does in [area]. Would you have 20 minutes to share your experience?" This conversation is often what puts you on the shortlist when a role opens up.

03

LinkedIn Outreach Done Right

Personalize every request. Mention where you saw their work, a shared interest, or a specific reason you're reaching out. Generic requests get ignored. Three genuine sentences unlock 80% of responses.

04

Industry Communities

Join Discord servers, Slack groups, Subreddits, and professional associations in your field. These are where jobs get shared informally — often before they're posted anywhere else.

5 Ways to Network Without Being Awkward

01

The Learning Ask (not the job ask)

Open with "I'd love to learn from your experience" — not "Can you refer me?" People want to help those who are curious. The referral comes naturally after relationship is built.

02

Target 3–5 Dream Companies

Map out your target companies before they post jobs. Find employees in your target role on LinkedIn. Build a relationship before you need it — not after.

03

Deliver Value First

Comment thoughtfully on their posts. Share a useful article. Congratulate them on a work milestone. Show up in their world before asking for anything. Give first.

04

Weak Ties Are Your Secret Weapon

Harvard research found that "weak ties" — loose connections, not close friends — are more likely to help you land jobs. Your hairdresser, old classmate, and former professor might be your best leads.

05

Ask for Introductions, Not Jobs

"I noticed you know [Person at Company X]. Would you be comfortable connecting us?" This is far more effective than asking for a job referral cold. Let the connection happen first.

The Proactive Phase
2

Find & Connect With Real Employees

Find 3–5 people at each target company in roles you aspire to. Connect with a personalized message. Mention their specific work. Don't ask for a job — ask for a conversation. "I've been following your team's work on [X] and would love to learn more about your experience there."

The Relationship Phase
3

After 2–3 touchpoints, ask for a 15–20 minute conversation. "I'm exploring opportunities in [area] and would love your perspective. Would you be open to a quick virtual coffee?" Most people say yes. This conversation alone can put you at the top of their mental shortlist for the next opening.

The Access Phase
4

Post once a week on LinkedIn — share what you're learning, a project you're working on, or a hot take on your industry. Comment thoughtfully on posts from people at your target companies. Your name becomes familiar before you apply. Recruiters find you, not the other way around.
