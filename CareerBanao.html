Most resumes don't get rejected because the candidate isn't qualified. They get rejected because the resume never communicates the qualification — buried under vague phrases, inconsistent formatting, and zero numbers. Recruiters and Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS) are scanning, not reading, and a resume that doesn't speak their language gets filtered out in seconds, no matter how good the candidate actually is.

This guide breaks down exactly how to fix that — whether you're a fresher writing your first resume, or an experienced professional trying to make ten years of work fit on one page.

Why Most Resumes Get Rejected Before a Human Even Reads Them

Over 75% of mid-size and large companies use an Applicant Tracking System to filter resumes before a recruiter sees them. The ATS scans your resume for keywords from the job description, parses your work history, and scores how closely you match. A resume with the right experience but the wrong formatting — tables, columns, headers in graphics, unusual fonts — can get misread or rejected automatically.

Quick Check

Save your resume as a .docx or simple PDF (not a designed image), use standard section headers like "Work Experience" and "Education," and avoid text boxes or multi-column layouts. If you're not sure your current resume is ATS-friendly, that's usually the first thing to fix before anything else.

The Resume Structure That Works in 2026

Recruiters scan resumes in a predictable pattern — name, most recent role, then skills. Structure your resume to match how it's actually read:

  1. Header with contact info Full name, phone number, professional email, city, and LinkedIn URL. Skip the photo, age, and marital status — they add clutter and, in many countries, can trigger bias filters that hurt rather than help you.
  2. Professional summary (2–3 lines) Not an objective. State who you are professionally, your strongest skill area, and the type of role you're targeting. This is prime real estate — write it last, after you know what the rest of the resume says.
  3. Core skills section A scannable list of 8–12 skills that match the job description's language exactly. This is where ATS keyword matching matters most.
  4. Work experience, most recent first Each role gets 3–5 bullet points. Lead with action verbs, end with measurable results. Skip duties — show outcomes.
  5. Education and certifications Degree, institution, year. Add relevant certifications (especially for technical or finance roles) above education if they're more relevant to the job than your degree.

Resume Tips for Freshers vs. Experienced Professionals

The advice changes depending on how much work history you have to work with. Here's what actually matters at each stage:

Freshers

No experience? Lead with proof of skill.

  • Put your education and projects above work experience — they're your strongest section right now.
  • Include internships, college projects, and freelance work as real experience — format them the same way you'd format a job.
  • Quantify projects: "Built a website used by 200+ students" beats "Built a website."
  • Add a skills section with tools and technologies, even ones learned in coursework or online courses.
  • One page only. No employer expects a fresher's resume to need two.
Experienced Professionals

Years of experience? Lead with impact, not tenure.

  • Cut anything older than 10–15 years unless it's directly relevant — recruiters care about recent, relevant impact.
  • Replace task lists with results: revenue grown, costs cut, teams led, timelines beaten.
  • Tailor your summary and top skills to each role — a generic resume sent to 50 jobs underperforms a tailored one sent to 10.
  • If you've changed industries or had a career gap, address it briefly in the summary rather than leaving the reader to guess.
  • Two pages is fine past a certain seniority — but every line still has to earn its place.

Resume Keywords: What to Use and Where

Keywords aren't about stuffing your resume with buzzwords — they're about mirroring the specific language of the job description, because that's what both the ATS and the human reader are scanning for.

  • Pull 8–10 key terms directly from the job posting (skills, tools, certifications, job titles)
  • Use both the abbreviation and full form once each — e.g. "Search Engine Optimization (SEO)"
  • Place keywords naturally in your summary, skills section, and bullet points — not as a hidden list
  • Match the exact job title language when your experience supports it ("Project Manager" vs. "Project Lead")
  • Avoid keyword stuffing — an ATS score that's too perfect can read as unnatural to the human reviewer who reads it next

7 Resume Mistakes That Quietly Cost You Interviews

  1. Listing duties instead of achievements. "Managed social media accounts" tells a recruiter nothing. "Grew Instagram following from 2K to 15K in 6 months" tells them everything.
  2. One generic resume for every application. A resume tailored to the specific job, even with 15 minutes of editing, consistently outperforms a one-size-fits-all version.
  3. Typos and inconsistent formatting. Mismatched date formats, inconsistent bullet styles, and spelling errors are read as a lack of attention to detail — often the very thing the job requires.
  4. Burying the most relevant experience. If your most relevant project is three jobs back, bring it forward in the summary instead of hoping the reader finds it.
  5. Using an unprofessional or outdated email address. A clean professional email takes two minutes to set up and removes an easy reason to be filtered out.
  6. No measurable results anywhere on the page. Numbers — percentages, amounts, timeframes, team sizes — are what separate a forgettable resume from a memorable one.
  7. Ignoring the cover letter. Even a short, well-written cover letter signals genuine interest and gives context a resume alone can't.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long should a resume be?

One page if you have under 8–10 years of experience. Two pages if you have more than that, or are in academia, medicine, or a senior leadership role. Recruiters spend an average of 6–7 seconds on a first pass, so every line has to earn its place.

What is an ATS-friendly resume?

An ATS-friendly resume is formatted so Applicant Tracking System software can read it correctly — simple structure, standard section headings, no text boxes, tables, columns, or graphics that hide content, and keywords from the job description used naturally throughout.

Should freshers use a resume objective or summary?

A short summary works better than a generic objective. In 2–3 lines, state your degree or field, your strongest skill area, and what kind of role you're targeting — written like a pitch, not a wish list.
