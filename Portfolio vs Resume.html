How a portfolio and resume actually differ

How a portfolio and resume actually differ

They serve complementary, not competing, purposes in most applications.

01

A resume summarizes; a portfolio demonstrates

Your resume states you "designed 30+ brand identities" — your portfolio shows exactly what those look like.

02

Portfolios are essential for creative/technical roles

Design, development, writing, and photography roles typically expect a linked portfolio alongside the resume.

03

A resume is still required even with a strong portfolio

Recruiters and ATS software still expect a resume for initial screening, regardless of portfolio strength.

Mistakes

Common portfolio and resume mistakes

✕

Portfolio link missing from the resume

Always place your portfolio link prominently near your name and contact details.

✕

Portfolio and resume telling different stories

Make sure the projects and results in your resume match what's shown in your portfolio.

✕

Outdated or broken portfolio links

Test every link before submitting applications — a broken portfolio link costs you the opportunity.

Pro tips

Pro tips for portfolio and resume together

Lead your resume with portfolio-worthy results

Choose resume bullets that map directly to strong pieces in your portfolio.

Keep the portfolio to your best 3-6 pieces

Quality over quantity — a curated portfolio outperforms an exhaustive one.

Update both together

Whenever you finish a strong project, update your resume and portfolio at the same time.

FAQ

Portfolio vs resume — FAQ

Do I need both a portfolio and a resume?

For creative and technical roles, yes — a resume alone often isn't sufficient proof of quality without a linked portfolio.

Where should I put my portfolio link?

In your resume header, near your name and contact information, so it's immediately visible.

What if I don't have a portfolio yet?

Start with 3-5 of your strongest existing projects, even personal or academic ones, and build from there.
