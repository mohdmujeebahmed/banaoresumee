common mistakes

What makes a skills section useless

  • ✕A wall of buzzwords. "Team player, hard worker, detail-oriented" with nothing behind it reads as filler and gets skimmed past.
  • ✕Skills copy-pasted from an old resume. Leftover skills from a past role that have nothing to do with this job dilute the ones that matter.
  • ✕Skill and job posting mismatch. Listing "customer service" when the posting says "client relationship management" costs you a keyword match.
  • ✕Skills with zero backup. Claiming a skill nowhere reflected in your experience section raises a red flag in an interview.
  • ✕Everything crammed into one line. An unreadable comma-separated wall makes it hard for a human or a bot to parse individual skills.
interview tips

Be ready to back up every skill you listed

01

Have one story per skill

For every skill on your resume, keep one specific example ready — what you did, what happened, what you'd do differently.

02

Expect a "walk me through it"

Interviewers often ask you to walk through a tool or process you listed step by step — rehearse it out loud, not just in your head.

03

Don't inflate proficiency

Listing "expert" in something you've used twice is an easy way to get caught out in a technical follow-up question.

04

Connect skills to their result

Whenever you mention a skill, tie it back to an outcome — it reframes a skill from a claim into evidence.
